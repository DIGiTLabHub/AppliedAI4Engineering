\section{AI by Far (Covered in This Class)}

\subsection{Relations of Classic AI and ML}
Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the broad field concerned with simulating aspects of human intelligence—reasoning, decision-making, perception—within computer systems.  Machine Learning (ML) is a subfield of AI that focuses on algorithms which \emph{learn} patterns from data rather than rely on hand-coded rules.

\colorbox{blue!10}{%
  \parbox{\textwidth}{%
    \textbf{Key Insight:} AI encompasses ML.  Whereas classical AI often used explicit, symbolic rules, ML relies on inductive reasoning to infer predictive models automatically from data.
  }%
}

\begin{figure}[h!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{./figs/Figure0103.png}
  \caption{Relationship between AI and ML: ML is the data-driven core within the broader AI discipline.}
  \label{fig:ai_ml_relationships}
\end{figure}


\subsection{Classic ML Methods Covered}
In this course we have surveyed the following foundational ML techniques:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Logistic Regression}  
    A linear model for binary classification, mapping inputs through the sigmoid function to estimate class probabilities.
  \item \textbf{Perceptron and MLPs}  
    Neural network models—single‐layer (perceptron) or multi‐layer (MLP)—that learn decision boundaries (classification) or continuous mappings (regression) via iterative weight updates.
  \item \textbf{Support Vector Machines (SVM)}  
    Max-margin classifiers (and regressors) that use kernels to handle nonlinearity while maintaining convex optimization.
  \item \textbf{Bayesian Classifiers}  
    Generative models (e.g.\ Naive Bayes) that apply Bayes’ theorem under conditional independence assumptions to compute class posteriors.
  \item \textbf{\(k\)-Means Clustering}  
    An unsupervised algorithm that partitions data into \(k\) groups by minimizing within-cluster variance.
  \item \textbf{Deep Neural Networks}  
    Multi-layer architectures (CNNs, RNNs, Transformers) that perform hierarchical, data-driven feature extraction and achieve state-of-the-art results across vision, language, and more.
\end{itemize}

\colorbox{red!10}{%
  \parbox{\textwidth}{%
    \textbf{All of these are data-driven models, which can be classified as:}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \emph{Connectionist:} Neural-network-based approaches (Perceptron, MLP, DNNs, etc.)  
      \item \emph{Statistical:} Probabilistic and kernel methods (SVMs, Bayesian classifiers, EM, etc.)
    \end{itemize}
  }%
}

\section{Motivation for GOFAI}

\subsection{Human Reasoning: Observations vs. Rules}

Humans learn not only by observing the world but also by internalizing formal rules—grammar, logic, and domain‐specific procedures—that guide reasoning beyond mere pattern recognition.

\colorbox{blue!10}{%
  \parbox{\textwidth}{%
    \textbf{Key Question:} Do humans reason based solely on observations (data)?%
  }%
}

Infants initially depend on sensory experience, yet very early in development they acquire language and formal rules that structure their understanding of the world.

\colorbox{blue!10}{%
  \parbox{\textwidth}{%
    \textbf{Early AI Motivation:} Pioneers such as Alan Turing (1950), John McCarthy (1955), Marvin Minsky (1956), and Herbert Simon \& Allen Newell (1956) argued that symbolic, rule‐based systems are needed to capture the reasoning processes characteristic of human intelligence.%
  }%
}

\subsection{Defining GOFAI: Basic Features}

GOFAI (Good‐Old‐Fashioned AI) is characterized by two core elements:

\paragraph{Symbolic Representation}  
Knowledge is encoded as discrete, human‐readable symbols.  
\[
  \texttt{Parent}(\text{John},\,\text{Mary})
  \quad\text{denotes}\quad
  \text{“John is a parent of Mary.”}
\]

\paragraph{Rule‐Based Reasoning}  
Inference is performed by applying explicit IF–THEN rules to symbolic facts.  
\[
  \text{IF }\texttt{Parent}(x,y)\;\text{AND}\;\texttt{Parent}(y,z)
  \quad\text{THEN}\quad
  \texttt{Grandparent}(x,z).
\]

\colorbox{yellow!10}{%
  \parbox{\textwidth}{%
    \textbf{Contrast with Data‐Driven ML:} Connectionist (neural) and statistical models learn patterns inductively from data, whereas GOFAI relies on explicitly encoded symbolic knowledge and rules to perform reasoning.%
  }%
}

\section{GOFAI Basics}

\subsection{Historical Milestones (1956–1990)}

The early era of symbolic AI featured groundbreaking systems and subsequent “AI winters”:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{1956: Logic Theorist (Newell \& Simon)}  
    First program to prove theorems in symbolic logic.
  \item \textbf{1957: General Problem Solver (Newell \& Simon)}  
    A universal problem‐solving architecture using means–ends analysis.
  \item \textbf{1970: SHRDLU (Winograd)}  
    Natural‐language understanding and block‐world manipulation.
  \item \textbf{mid‐1970s: AI Winter I}  
    Funding cuts after overly optimistic early results.
  \item \textbf{1980s: Expert Systems Boom}  
    Industry adoption (e.g.\ XCON, DENDRAL, PROSPECTOR).
  \item \textbf{late‐1980s–early‐1990s: AI Winter II}  
    Collapse of the expert-systems market.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Resurgence \& Semantic Era (1990–2024)}

Symbolic methods were complemented and sometimes supplanted by statistical and hybrid approaches:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{1990s:} Revival of neural networks (Rumelhart et al., 1986), introduction of SVMs (Cortes \& Vapnik, 1995), graphical models.
  \item \textbf{2000s:} Semantic Web \& Knowledge Graphs (RDF/OWL, SPARQL).
  \item \textbf{2010s:} Deep Learning revolution (CNNs, Transformers).
  \item \textbf{2020s:} Generative AI (large‐scale LLMs, diffusion models) and emergence of neurosymbolic frameworks.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Knowledge Representation}

Symbolic AI supports multiple formalisms for encoding structured knowledge:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Frames \& Objects:}  
    Records with named slots, e.g.\ \(\texttt{Person(name, age, occupation)}\).  
    Inheritance: \(\texttt{Student}\sqsubseteq\texttt{Person}\).
  \item \textbf{Semantic Networks:}  
    Directed graphs with labeled edges, e.g.\ \(\texttt{Dog}\xrightarrow{\texttt{isA}}\texttt{Mammal}\).  
    Inference via transitive closure.
  \item \textbf{Ontologies (Description Logics):}  
    Concept inclusion \(Dog\sqsubseteq Animal\), role restrictions \(Dog\sqsubseteq \exists hasPart.\,Tail\).  
    Trade‐off: expressivity vs.\ reasoning complexity (e.g.\ \(\mathcal{ALC}\) vs.\ \(\mathcal{SROIQ}\)).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Predicate Logic}

Logical statements encode precise facts and inference rules:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Clauses:}  
    \[
      C_1: P(a)\lor Q(a)
      \quad
      C_2: \neg P(a)
    \]
  \item \textbf{Resolution Rule:}
    \[
      \frac{L \lor \phi,\;\neg L \lor \psi}{\phi \lor \psi}
    \]
  \item \textbf{Example:}
    \[
      \frac{P(a)\lor Q(a),\;\neg P(a)}{Q(a)}
    \]
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Unification}

Unification finds substitutions to make terms identical:

\[
  \mathrm{mgu}\bigl(f(x,a),\,f(b,y)\bigr)
  = \{x\mapsto b,\;y\mapsto a\}.
\]

Applying these yields:
\[
  f(x,a)\to f(b,a),
  \quad
  f(b,y)\to f(b,a).
\]

\subsection{Search \& Production Systems}

Symbolic inference often involves search and rule firing:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Search Algorithms:}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item BFS, DFS for tree/graph search.
    \item A\*: \(f(n)=g(n)+h(n)\) with admissible heuristic.
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Production Systems (Rete Algorithm):}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Working memory \(WM\), rule set \(R\).
    \item Update: \(WM_{t+1}=WM_t\cup\{\mathrm{head}(r)\mid \mathrm{body}(r)\subseteq WM_t\}\).
    \item Rete compiles rules into a shared-test network giving \(O(\Delta WM + \Delta R)\) matching per update.
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\section{Expert Systems}

\subsection{Definition and Origins}
An \emph{expert system} is a software application that encodes expert knowledge in a domain as a set of symbolic rules and uses an inference engine to draw conclusions.

\colorbox{green!10}{%
  \parbox{\textwidth}{%
    \textbf{Architecture:}  
    \(\bullet\) Knowledge Base (facts + rules)  
    \(\bullet\) Inference Engine (forward/backward chaining)  
    \(\bullet\) Explanation Facility (justifies conclusions)
  }%
}

Early pioneering systems:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{DENDRAL} (Feigenbaum \& Buchanan, 1965): Chemical-structure elucidation  
  \item \textbf{MYCIN} (Buchanan \& Shortliffe, 1972): Medical diagnosis with certainty factors  
  \item Term “Expert System” popularized in the mid-1970s at Stanford and Carnegie Mellon.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Expert Systems Hype}
Expert systems generated enormous excitement and investment in the 1980s:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Promise of Automation:} Capture scarce human expertise (medicine, engineering, finance).
  \item \textbf{Business Impact:}  
    \(\bullet\) Consistent, 24/7 decision support  
    \(\bullet\) Example: XCON at DEC saved over \$40 million per year.
  \item \textbf{Funding Surge:} Governments and industry invested billions in knowledge‐based initiatives.
  \item \textbf{Bottlenecks Uncovered:} Knowledge‐acquisition, brittleness, difficulty adapting to new domains.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Applications in Engineering Design}
Notable engineering expert systems:
\begin{itemize}
  \item XCON (DEC, 1980): Automatic VAX configuration  
  \item PROSPECTOR (Stanford, 1978): Geologic exploration guidance  
  \item ICAD (MIT, 1988): Parametric CAD for mechanical parts  
  \item CADEX/KEE (Intel, early 1980s): Advisory shells for engineering tasks  
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Case Study: HI-RISE (CMU, 1985–1987)}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Challenge:} Structural design problems are often ill‐structured and rely on expert heuristics.
  \item \textbf{Approach:}  
    \(\bullet\) Encode design rules and heuristics in a symbolic knowledge base  
    \(\bullet\) Use forward/backward chaining to derive design decisions  
    \(\bullet\) Integrate case‐based reasoning to adapt previous examples
  \item \textbf{Impact:}  
    Pioneered rule‐based support for structural member sizing and load analysis; influenced later CAD‐integrated design assistants.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations and Criticisms}
Expert systems revealed key drawbacks:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Brittleness \& Frame Problem:} Rigid rule bases fail with novel scenarios.
  \item \textbf{Knowledge‐Acquisition Bottleneck:} Manual extraction and encoding of expert rules is time‐consuming.
  \item \textbf{Scalability \& Maintenance:} Large rule sets become difficult to update and debug.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusions}

In this lecture, we explored the evolution and principles of Good‐Old‐Fashioned AI (GOFAI) and its relationship to modern, data‐driven methods:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{GOFAI’s Strengths:}  
    Transparent, human‐readable rule bases with strong guarantees on logical consistency and explainability.
  \item \textbf{Expert Systems’ Impact:}  
    Pioneering domain automation (e.g., DENDRAL, MYCIN, XCON) but challenged by knowledge‐acquisition bottlenecks and maintenance as rule bases grew.
  \item \textbf{Neuro‐Symbolic Integration:}  
    Hybrid frameworks (LogicTensorNetworks, DeepProbLog, Neuro‐Symbolic Transformers) seek to combine symbolic constraints with statistical learning for robust, explainable AI.
  \item \textbf{Path toward AGI/AMI:}  
    Scalable neuro‐symbolic architectures—modular “cognitive stacks” with integrated symbolic audit trails and statistical confidence—offer a promising route to AI systems with human‐level adaptability, commonsense reasoning, and verifiable safety.
\end{itemize}

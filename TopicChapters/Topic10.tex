%\section{Overview}
Having completed our study of supervised discriminative and generative learning, we are now in a position to examine model performance beyond apparent accuracy or model capacity measured on the currently available training and testing data. In real-world applications, the more important question is how a model behaves on \emph{unseen} data.

This leads directly to the notion of \textbf{generalization}. A model may appear strong on known data yet still fail to generalize due to \textbf{underfitting} or \textbf{overfitting}. Closely related to this challenge is \textbf{model selection}: deciding thresholds, hyperparameters, and even model families in ways that improve performance robustness rather than only fitting the present dataset.

\section{Generalization}
\subsection{Under- or Over-fitting}
In ML practice, for a classification problem, a simple train-test process is typically used: (1) train a model on a labeled training dataset, denoted by \(\mathcal{D}_{tr}\); and (2) test the trained model on a labeled testing dataset, denoted by \(\mathcal{D}_{te}\). For the testing phase, we have learned various model performance measures, including overall accuracy, precision, recall, and even a graphical ROC curve.

This is a valid process; however, practical issues arise.

First of all, training and testing datasets—due to their collection and labeling processes—are often expensive in terms of operational and labor costs. In many real applications, the training and testing data are provided in one batch—namely, a labeled dataset,
\[
\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, c_i) \mid i = 1, \cdots, N\},
\]
where \(N\) is finite.

Given such a constraint, we need to split the data into two parts, namely \(\mathcal{D}_{tr}\) and \(\mathcal{D}_{te}\). With an arbitrary split that satisfies this ML process (training and testing), good—even nearly perfect—performance estimates may be obtained. But are these performance measurements trustworthy? What if the classification accuracy drops significantly when different testing data are chosen later? In such a scenario, we state that the obtained model has low generalization performance.

In the following, we first introduce the concept of generalization and then describe empirical techniques to improve it.

In machine learning, generalization refers to a model's ability to perform well on unseen data by accurately predicting or classifying outcomes based on its learned knowledge from the existing data. Qualitatively, a model that generalizes well can effectively capture the underlying true patterns and relationships without overfitting or underfitting.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Overfitting} occurs when a model learns the noise or random fluctuations present in the training data, resulting in high performance on the training data but poor performance on new, unseen data.
    \item \textbf{Underfitting} happens when a model is too simple to capture the underlying patterns in the data, leading to suboptimal performance on both training and unseen data.
\end{itemize}

The goal of a machine-learning model is to strike the right balance between fitting the training data and maintaining good performance on unseen data. The dilemma is that truly unseen data are unavailable during development; in practice, we work with a given dataset split into training and testing subsets. So, how do we improve a model's generalization performance? Effective empirical techniques include model regularization, data resampling, model selection, and ensembling.

Previously, we learned that a Support Vector Machine has a native analytical regularization mechanism; for other models, we may explicitly introduce a regularization term during training (by modifying its objective loss function). In the following, we focus on two resampling methods: cross-validation and bootstrapping, which operate on the given training and testing data. Then, we discuss model selection and ensemble learning, which operate at the model level.

A deeper understanding of generalization requires examining the \textbf{bias-variance trade-off}. This trade-off is central to understanding overfitting and underfitting:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Bias} refers to the error introduced by approximating a complex real-world problem with a simpler model. High bias may force a model to miss important relationships between features and the target, leading to \emph{underfitting}.
    \item \textbf{Variance} refers to the error that arises from a model's sensitivity to small fluctuations in the training data. High variance can cause a model to learn noise as if it were a true signal, resulting in \emph{overfitting}.
\end{itemize}

Striking the right balance is crucial. A model with high bias and low variance tends to underfit the data, while a model with low bias and high variance tends to overfit. Typical bias--variance plots (see Figures~\ref{fig:bias-variance-01} and~\ref{fig:bias-variance-02}) present different visual forms of the same idea: how training and test errors vary with model complexity. These figures emphasize that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item As model complexity increases, bias typically decreases.
    \item As model complexity increases beyond a certain point, variance increases sharply, which causes overfitting.
\end{itemize}


\section{Re-sampling Methods}
Cross-validation is an essential machine-learning technique used to evaluate a \textit{selected} model's performance through repeated training and testing. More specifically, cross-validation is useful in three aspects:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Achieving more robust performance measurements:} Cross-validation helps estimate how well a model will perform on new, unseen data. By dividing the dataset into multiple train-test folds, it provides a more robust estimate of performance based on the available data and better reflects performance on unseen data.
    
    \item \textbf{Preventing overfitting/underfitting:} Overfitting occurs when a model learns the noise in the training data rather than the underlying pattern. This results in deceptively high performance on the training data but poor performance on new data. Cross-validation helps mitigate this issue by evaluating the model on different subsets of data, providing a more accurate estimate of its generalization performance.
    
    \item \textbf{Model selection:} Cross-validation provides a way to compare different models or hyperparameter settings by assessing their performance on various data subsets, allowing for informed decisions on model selection and hyperparameter tuning. On the other hand, the concept of model selection has a broader meaning, and there are other methods that can be used for model selection.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{K-Fold Cross-Validation}
K-fold cross-validation is a widely used method for implementing cross-validation. It involves the following steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\textbf{1)}] \textit{Partition}: The dataset is partitioned into \(K\) subsets, each containing an equal number of data points (or as close to equal as possible).
    \item[\textbf{2)}] \textit{Iterative training and testing}: Given the \(K\) subsets, iteratively pick one subset as the testing set, and use the remaining \(K-1\) subsets for training.
    \item[\textbf{3)}] \textit{Compute model accuracy}: The performance metric (e.g., overall accuracy, F1 score, etc.) is calculated at each iteration, and the average performance is computed over all iterations.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Bootstrapping}
We have covered resampling methods, and K-fold cross-validation is one of the widely used methods. The other common approach is called bootstrapping.

Bootstrapping is a resampling technique used to assess the performance of an ML model. It involves randomly sampling a dataset \emph{with replacement} to create multiple bootstrap samples, each of which is used to train and evaluate a model.

The idea behind bootstrapping is that by repeatedly sampling from the original dataset, we can create multiple independent datasets similar to the original dataset in terms of their statistical properties. We can then use each of these datasets to train and evaluate separate models and average the results to estimate the model's performance on the original dataset.

Bootstrapping differs from cross-validation in how it creates datasets. Bootstrapping randomly samples the original dataset with replacement to create multiple independent datasets, whereas cross-validation involves splitting the original dataset into multiple non-overlapping subsets or "folds."

Another operational difference is how they handle dataset size. Bootstrapping can be helpful when we have a limited dataset, but it can be computationally expensive for large datasets. Cross-validation, on the other hand, is more efficient for large datasets and can provide a more accurate estimate of a model's performance.

\section{Model Selection}
There are three scenarios where we encounter a model selection problem:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Predicting Probabilities and Classification Threshold Selection.} A model is trained with a selected architecture and all model-level parameters are user-defined (e.g., the number of hidden layers and type of activation functions in an MLP). In this case (i.e., binary classification), a predicted label is determined by the threshold used in the model. The threshold is often associated with the predicted probability of a class label. How should such a threshold be chosen?
    
    \item \textbf{Hyper-parameter Tuning.} A model is trained with a selected architecture (e.g., an MLP or an SVM); however, there are one or more parameters chosen by the modeler (user). These parameters are called hyperparameters.
    
    \item \textbf{Use of Multiple Different Models.} A number of different models are trained with different architectures (e.g., an MLP, an SVM, a decision tree, etc.). In this setting, the user must decide which model to use, or whether multiple models should be combined to improve accuracy and generalization.
\end{enumerate}

These cases are further elaborated below.

\subsection{Classification Threshold Selection}
In a classification problem, once the model function \(f(u)\) is selected (where \(u(\mathbf{x})\) defines the decision boundary), the class label can be predicted directly (e.g., in a perceptron or MLP with a hard-threshold output) or inferred from a score. In the Logistic Regression model, the function is the sigmoid function, which always varies from 0 to 1.

In practice, even if it is a discriminative classification model (i.e., probability distributions are not explicitly modeled), one can treat the sigmoid function output as a probability measure. In this case, \(P(c|\mathbf{x})\) is set equal to \(f(u)\); often \(P(c|\mathbf{x})\) is called the class-membership probability (or score) for label \(c\).

When using a hard-threshold function (e.g., in a linear SVM or in the last activation of an MLP), or when the output is not between 0 and 1, methods are available to estimate class-membership probabilities. In practice, most classification models can be calibrated to output probability-like scores.

If there is no preference over the type of false prediction, one may simply use a threshold of 0.5 to decide the class label:
\[
c = \begin{cases} 
      1 & P(c|\mathbf{x}) \geq \frac{1}{2} \\
      0 & P(c|\mathbf{x}) < \frac{1}{2} 
      \end{cases}
\]

However, in real applications, we may need to prioritize avoiding one type of error (false positives or false negatives). In this case, the threshold can be adjusted accordingly. For example, in medical practice (e.g., diagnosing cancer for a patient), a "miss" (false negative, which can have life-threatening consequences) is considered more severe than a "false alarm" (false positive, which may cause temporary physiological anxiety). In such a case, the threshold for a "positive" label should be lower.

To compare model performance under such variable thresholds, common methods include two graphical tools: the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve and the Precision-Recall (PR) curve.

\subsection{Hyper-parameter Tuning}
In practice, an ML classification model typically has one or more high-level parameters, or hyperparameters. Recall that in a Logistic Regression model or in a kernel SVM model, we can change the \(C\) value to regularize the model. Moreover, we can choose different kernels, and such selections (decision variables) are considered hyperparameters. Even when a kernel is chosen, there are kernel parameters (e.g., the kernel width in an RBF kernel or the degree in a polynomial kernel).

In this case, we have a model selection problem, or equivalently, we must determine which hyperparameter combination yields a more capable model.

The aforementioned ROC and PR curves can be used in this situation as well.

\subsection{Models with Different Architectures}
It is straightforward to understand this situation — namely, when a user tries different models. The deeper rationale is that ML models come with different levels of complexity---so how do we choose among them?

There are some philosophical perspectives on this problem. In philosophy, there is the so-called Occam's razor principle, or more formally, the law of parsimony. In layman's words, this law states that if there are multiple explanations for a phenomenon, the simplest explanation is usually the correct one. In modern probability and statistics (the foundation of ML), the Occam's razor principle is considered a direct consequence of basic probability. Basically, all assumptions (via modeling) introduce possibilities for error; if an assumption does not improve the accuracy of a theory, its only effect is to increase the probability that the overall model is wrong. In other words, excessive complexity, which means more assumptions, is not necessary.

To achieve a balance between modeling complexity and accuracy, two categories of techniques are often used:
\begin{itemize} 
    \item Resampling methods, using the aforementioned cross-validation or bootstrapping methods.
    \item Probability measures, as shown below.
\end{itemize}

Probability measures are defined to score candidate models using both their performance on the training dataset and the complexity of the model. Four commonly used probabilistic model selection measures include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)
    \item Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)
    \item Minimum Description Length (MDL)
    \item Structural Risk Minimization (SRM)
\end{itemize}
We will not cover these topics in this class; they are mentioned here for awareness.

On the other hand, when models with different architectures are available, we may use ensemble machine learning instead of selecting only one model. The idea is to aggregate predictions from multiple models to achieve improved performance. 

\section{Ensemble Models}
If a set of models is selected, having different degrees of complexity, do we pick one model or possibly use all of them? The rationale is that the simplest model and the most complex model each have their own advantages; after all, we cannot fully foresee what ``unseen'' data will be like.

Ensemble modeling in machine learning is a technique that involves combining multiple individual models to improve the overall predictive power of the system. The idea behind ensemble modeling is that by combining the predictions of several weak models, the final result will be more accurate and robust than any individual model could achieve on its own.

There are different types of ensemble models, including:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Bagging:} This technique involves creating multiple models using the same algorithm on different subsets of the training data, and then aggregating their predictions.
    \item \textbf{Boosting:} This technique involves creating multiple models sequentially, with each new model focusing on the examples that were misclassified by the previous model.
    \item \textbf{Stacking:} This technique involves training several different models on the same data and using their predictions as input to a higher-level model that learns how to combine them best.
\end{itemize}

Finally, ensemble modeling is beneficial when dealing with complex datasets, where individual models may struggle to capture all the underlying patterns and relationships in unseen data. By combining multiple models, ensemble modeling can help reduce overfitting and increase the system's overall accuracy.

In this topic, we explored the challenges of generalization in machine learning and presented various empirical techniques to enhance model performance on unseen data. The discussion included resampling methods, such as K-fold cross-validation and bootstrapping, which provide robust performance estimates, as well as model selection strategies—including threshold selection, hyperparameter tuning, and comparing models with different complexities. We also reviewed ensemble methods as a means to combine multiple models, thereby reducing overfitting and improving overall predictive accuracy. Together, these techniques form a comprehensive framework for developing machine learning models that generalize well to real-world scenarios.
